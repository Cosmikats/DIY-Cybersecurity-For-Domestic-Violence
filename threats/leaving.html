I want to leave my partner.

Whether to leave a relationship is your decision. It may depend on your physical safety, the safety of your children, the financial resources available to you, immigration status, work opportunities, and a myriad of other factors. You know what's best for you. Before reading this guide, consider reaching out to a domestic violence organization for support.

This guide will focus on technological considerations when leaving a relationship. Securing your phone and computer protects your communication with your support networks. Using strong, unique passwords for email, bank accounts, healthcare accounts, and other important online services can prevent your partner from sabotaging your livelihood. Backing up important data, such as document files or photos, provides a safety-net in case you lose control of your personal devices or accounts. By taking steps to secure your digital life, you are asserting your independence.

Create a safety plan

Knowing how you will leave your partner is a very valuable form of preparation. Because technology can be so complex, spread across devices and websites, it is helpful to create a technology safety plan before taking action. You can consider vulnerabilities in your data, potential threats to your devices, and other factors that could affect the separation from your partner. We will explore several strategies that mitigate the risk of your partner compromising your digital assets. Then, you can make informed decisions on what security measures make the most sense for you.

Use devices you know are not being monitored

If you know or suspect your partner to be monitoring your online activity, consult our guide "My partner is monitoring my computer or cell phone activity" before taking further action. You want to make sure that you are leaving the relationship on your own terms, especially if you afraid of your partner's reaction. When searching for support online, use a private browser window that does not record browsing history, or use a public computer at a library.

Secure your online accounts

Your partner may have access to your important online accounts, such as email, banking, and healthcare. The best way to protect these accounts is to follow our PASSWORD and TWO-FACTOR AUTHENTICATION defense strategies: a strong, unique password, combined with two-factor authentication, means that your partner will be unable to access your accounts. Make a mental list of your most important accounts and apply these defense strategies starting with the most important. The passwords do not need to be perfect in this scenario: the most crucial point is that your partner does not know them and could not guess them.

Implementing these strategies depends on when you feel safe enough to do so. If your partner does not regularly check these accounts, you can secure them when you feel comfortable. If they are likely to notice immediately, you can secure your accounts soon after you have physically separated and know that you are safe.

Back-up important data

An abusive partner may attempt to seize or destroy your electronic devices in an attempt to assert control. They might try to delete incriminating text conversations, photographs, or videos. Budgets, tax information, healthcare documents, and work emails may also be targeted.

Make a mental list of the data on your computer that you want to protect. When you have the opportunity, back-up the files into a cloud service your partner does not have access to, such as a new Dropbox account. Be aware that uploading files may take a long period of time. Alternatively, buy an external hard-drive that you can connect to your computer via USB. You can simply copy your important files to the hard-drive and take it with you. Because your most important data is at stake, it is not excessive to use both strategies.

Backing-up data from your cell-phone depends on the apps you are using and whether your partner has access to your cloud. Text message conversations are not always backed-up in the cloud, so take screenshots of anything you want to preserve. Send important photos, videos, and contacts to a secure email address. You can also send them to your friends or family to protect them for you. If your partner has access to your cloud, it is especially important that your data be preserved outside of that cloud, even if you intend on removing their access.

Disable any location-tracking on your devices

At some point, you may have enabled GPS location sharing on your phone or laptop. Many apps "check in" online, sharing your location to other users. Some laptops have a "find my computer" option that can provide location in case of theft. It's easy to forget about these settings, so check your devices and websites to see where your location may be shared. Either remove location permissions from these applications, or disable location services entirely. That way, your whereabouts will be private if your partner attempts to locate you.

Conclusion

Leaving a relationship is incredibly difficult, through no fault of your own. When you are preparing to leave, create a technology safety plan: use devices that are not being monitored by your partner, secure your online accounts with strong passwords and/or two-factor authentication, back-up your most important data (even if you already have a cloud), and disable location tracking on your devices and websites. Your privacy is not just a right, it's an asset that will protect your autonomy.
